# German Weapons — Normandy 1944

Reference material for game content. Every entry answers: **what does facing this weapon mean for the player's decisions?**

These are the weapons an American paratrooper platoon would encounter in the Normandy hedgerow country, June 1944.

---

## MG-42 (Maschinengewehr 42)

**The single most important weapon on the Normandy battlefield.** Everything else is secondary to understanding this gun.

| Spec | Value |
|------|-------|
| Caliber | 7.92×57mm Mauser |
| Weight | 25.5 lbs (11.6 kg) with bipod |
| Effective range | 600 m bipod; 2,000 m on tripod with optic |
| Rate of fire | 1,100–1,200 rpm cyclic; 150–200 rpm sustained |
| Feed | 50 or 250-round belts |
| Muzzle velocity | 2,480 fps (755 m/s) |
| Crew | 2–3 (gunner, loader, ammo bearer) |

The MG-42's cyclic rate of 1,200 rpm is so fast that individual shots are indistinguishable — it produces a continuous tearing sound, earning it the names "Hitler's Buzzsaw," "Hitler's Zipper," and from American troops, simply "the burp gun." By comparison, the American M1919 fires at 400–550 rpm.

**What this means for the player:**

1. **Open ground is death.** A single MG-42 with clear fields of fire can suppress an entire platoon. Crossing open ground against an emplaced MG-42 without smoke, suppressive fire, or flanking is suicide. The player must treat every open space as a potential kill zone.

2. **Sound identification matters.** The MG-42's distinctive ripping sound is immediately recognizable. When the player hears it, the first response must be: get down, locate the source, do NOT advance into its beaten zone.

3. **The barrel change is your window.** At sustained rates, the MG-42 overheats its barrel in 150–250 rounds. A trained crew can swap barrels in 6–10 seconds. An untrained crew takes longer. That barrel change is the moment to move — the only pause in its fire.

4. **Flanking is mandatory.** The MG-42's beaten zone is narrow and deep. Moving laterally to its cone of fire is far safer than advancing into it. German doctrine positions MG-42s with interlocking fields of fire specifically to prevent flanking — the player must identify whether there's a second MG covering the flank of the first.

5. **German squads are built around this gun.** Unlike American squads where the BAR supplements rifle fire, German squads exist to feed, protect, and reposition the MG-42. The 6–8 riflemen in a German squad are there to carry ammunition and provide security for the MG team. Killing or suppressing the MG-42 crew guts the squad's combat power.

**In the hedgerows:** Germans typically positioned MG-42s at gaps in the bocage, at road junctions, and in upper floors of farmhouses. The thick earthen banks of hedgerows provided natural defilade. The gun could sweep any open field or lane between hedgerows. American platoons learned to never use roads or obvious paths — the MG-42 would be pre-registered on them.

## Karabiner 98k (Kar98k)

The standard German infantry rifle. Every German soldier who isn't carrying a specialized weapon has this.

| Spec | Value |
|------|-------|
| Caliber | 7.92×57mm Mauser |
| Weight | 8.6 lbs (3.9 kg) |
| Effective range | 500 m (iron sights); 800+ m (scoped) |
| Rate of fire | 10–15 aimed rounds/min |
| Feed | 5-round internal magazine, stripper clips |
| Muzzle velocity | 2,493 fps (760 m/s) |
| Overall length | 43.7 inches (1,110 mm) |

**What this means for the player:**

The Kar98k is accurate but slow. Its bolt-action mechanism means a German rifleman fires roughly half the volume of an M1 Garand-armed American. In a straight rifle-vs-rifle fight at equal numbers, American squads have the fire superiority advantage.

**But the Germans know this.** German doctrine compensates for inferior individual rifle firepower by concentrating everything on the MG-42. The riflemen's job is not to win firefights — it's to protect the machine gun and move ammunition. The Kar98k is dangerous to the player primarily because it means there's a German soldier present — and where there's one, there's an MG-42 nearby.

**Sniper variant:** Select Kar98k rifles with ZF39 or ZF42 telescopic sights served as sniper weapons. In hedgerow country, German snipers were a persistent threat, picking off officers, NCOs, and radiomen. If the player's leadership is being specifically targeted, a sniper is likely present.

## MP-40 (Maschinenpistole 40)

German submachine gun. Issued to squad leaders, vehicle crews, and Fallschirmjäger (paratroopers).

| Spec | Value |
|------|-------|
| Caliber | 9×19mm Parabellum |
| Weight | 8.75 lbs (4.0 kg) empty; 9.7 lbs loaded |
| Effective range | 100–200 m |
| Rate of fire | 500–550 rpm cyclic |
| Feed | 32-round box magazine |
| Muzzle velocity | 1,312 fps (400 m/s) |
| Overall length | 32.8 in extended; 24.8 in folded |

**What this means for the player:**

The MP-40 signals close-range danger. Its moderate rate of fire (500 rpm, slower than the Thompson) makes it controllable, and 9mm carries better at range than .45 ACP. But it's still a close-range weapon.

**Hearing MP-40 fire means the enemy is nearby** — within 100 meters at most for effective engagement. If the player is in a hedgerow fight and hears the distinctive slower *chatter* of the MP-40 (vs. the MG-42's rip), that's likely a squad leader or an assault element closing distance.

In Normandy, MP-40s were common among German NCOs and in Fallschirmjäger units defending Carentan. They're a CQB threat — dangerous in villages, buildings, and ambushes but not a major concern at ranges beyond 150m.

## Panzerfaust

The weapon American tankers feared and paratroopers needed to understand.

| Variant | Panzerfaust 30 | Panzerfaust 60 |
|---------|---------------|----------------|
| Weight | ~5.2 kg (11.5 lbs) | 6.25 kg (13.8 lbs) |
| Warhead | 149mm shaped charge | 149mm shaped charge |
| Effective range | 30 m | 60 m |
| Penetration | ~140mm armor | ~200mm armor |
| Operation | Single-shot, disposable | Single-shot, disposable |

By mid-1944, both the Panzerfaust 30 and 60 were in widespread use. The number indicates effective range in meters.

**What this means for the player:**

1. **Any German soldier can be anti-tank.** The Panzerfaust requires zero training — pull the pin, aim, press the trigger. It's cheap, disposable, and distributed liberally. Unlike the Bazooka, which requires a trained two-man team, the Panzerfaust turns every German rifleman into a potential tank killer.

2. **Extreme short range.** 30–60 meters is practically point-blank. The German soldier firing a Panzerfaust must be very close to his target and very exposed. This means ambush positions — hedgerow gaps, building windows, roadside ditches. The player should expect Panzerfaust ambushes at choke points.

3. **For the paratrooper (who has no tanks):** The Panzerfaust matters because captured ones become your own anti-armor capability. Airborne units routinely used captured Panzerfausts. If the player captures a German position, looting Panzerfausts is tactically sound — they're simpler and more effective at close range than the Bazooka.

4. **Against the player's own positions:** A Panzerfaust fired into a building or foxhole is devastating. It's not just anti-armor — it's anti-fortification. Germans used them to breach walls and destroy defensive positions.

## Stielhandgranate (Model 24 Stick Grenade)

The iconic German "potato masher."

| Spec | Value |
|------|-------|
| Weight | 1.3 lbs (0.6 kg) |
| Explosive | 6 oz (170 g) TNT |
| Fuse delay | 4–5 seconds |
| Blast radius | 12–15 yards |
| Throwing range | 30–40 yards (farther than Allied grenades due to handle leverage) |
| Overall length | 14 inches (360 mm) |

**What this means for the player:**

The stick grenade is a **concussion weapon**, not a fragmentation weapon. Its thin steel casing produces less shrapnel than the American Mk 2 pineapple grenade. This means:

- **Deadly in enclosed spaces** (buildings, bunkers, trenches) where the blast overpressure is lethal
- **Less lethal in open ground** than the Mk 2, which throws iron fragments in all directions
- **The handle allows longer throws** — German soldiers could lob stick grenades farther than Americans could throw pineapples, giving them an edge in grenade duels

When the player hears the distinctive clatter of a stick grenade landing nearby, they have 4–5 seconds. In a foxhole, that's enough time to throw it back or scramble. In a building room, it may not be.

**Geballte Ladung (bundle charge):** German soldiers sometimes wired 6–7 stick grenades together with the center grenade's fuse active. This improvised satchel charge was used against tanks, bunkers, and fortified positions. Devastating blast, but the thrower had to get very close.

## MG-34 (Maschinengewehr 34)

The MG-42's predecessor, still widely deployed in Normandy 1944.

| Spec | Value |
|------|-------|
| Caliber | 7.92×57mm Mauser |
| Weight | 26.7 lbs (12.1 kg) |
| Effective range | 600 m bipod; 2,000+ m tripod |
| Rate of fire | 800–900 rpm cyclic; ~150 rpm sustained |
| Feed | 50 or 250-round belt, or 50/75-round drum |
| Muzzle velocity | 2,510 fps (765 m/s) |
| Crew | 2–3 |

**What this means for the player:**

The MG-34 is nearly as dangerous as the MG-42. Its lower rate of fire (800–900 rpm vs 1,200 rpm) makes individual shots slightly more distinguishable — experienced players can tell the difference by sound. But it's still a belt-fed, general-purpose machine gun that dominates open ground.

**Key differences from MG-42 for gameplay:**
- Slightly slower rate of fire means slightly less suppressive effect
- More mechanically complex, slightly more prone to stoppages
- Can use drum magazines (75-round Patronentrommel 34) in addition to belts — relevant for vehicle-mounted or AA positions
- Same ammunition, same range, same lethality per round

In Normandy, players encounter MG-34s in the same roles as MG-42s: emplaced in hedgerows, in bunkers, mounted on vehicles, and in fixed defensive positions. Treat them with the same respect. The tactical response is identical: suppress, flank, close.

**Vehicle mounts:** The MG-34 was commonly mounted on tanks, halftracks, and armored vehicles as a hull or coaxial weapon. Encountering vehicle-mounted MG-34 fire means armored vehicles are present — a much bigger problem than the machine gun itself.

## 8cm Granatwerfer 34 (GrW 34) — Medium Mortar

The standard German infantry mortar.

| Spec | Value |
|------|-------|
| Caliber | 81.4mm |
| Weight | 125 lbs (57 kg) — breaks into 3 loads |
| Range | 400–1,200 m effective; 2,400 m maximum |
| Rate of fire | 15–25 rounds/min |
| Shell weight | 7.7 lbs (3.5 kg) |
| Crew | 3–5 men |

**What this means for the player:**

Mortars are the weapon the player cannot see, cannot directly engage, and cannot hide from behind a hedgerow. Unlike machine guns and rifles, mortar fire drops from above — foxholes and walls provide protection, but open positions and rooftops do not.

1. **Indirect fire changes everything.** When mortar rounds start landing, the player must either advance out of the beaten zone, retreat out of it, or dig in and endure. Staying static in the open under mortar fire is catastrophic.

2. **The observer is the target.** German mortars rely on forward observers to adjust fire. The observer is usually within 200–500m of the player's position, watching with binoculars. Killing or suppressing the observer is more practical than finding the mortar (which is 500–1,200m behind the front).

3. **Rate of fire is punishing.** At 15–25 rounds per minute, a single mortar tube puts a shell on your position every 2–4 seconds. Multiple tubes firing together create a continuous barrage that forces all movement to stop.

4. **Sound warning:** Experienced soldiers can hear the *thump* of mortar firing and the whistle of incoming rounds. There's a 3–8 second window between the thump and impact. In-game, this audio cue gives the player a brief moment to react — yell "incoming" and hit the dirt.

5. **Pre-registered targets:** German mortar crews pre-registered fire on road junctions, bridges, rally points, and obvious assembly areas. The first rounds land accurately with no adjustment needed. Moving through predictable terrain features invites immediate mortar fire.

## German Defensive Doctrine: How These Weapons Work Together

Understanding individual weapons is insufficient. German defensive positions in Normandy combined them into interlocking systems:

### The Hedgerow Defense

A typical German hedgerow strongpoint:

1. **MG-42 (or MG-34)** positioned at a gap or corner in the bocage with clear field of fire across the next open field. Interlocking with a second MG covering its flanks.
2. **Riflemen (Kar98k)** along the hedgerow bank, protecting the MG's flanks and watching for infiltrators.
3. **MP-40-armed NCO** directing fire, positioned to counter close-range flanking attempts.
4. **Mortar (GrW 34)** 500–1,000m behind the hedgerow line, pre-registered on the open field in front of the MG position.
5. **Panzerfausts** cached at the position for anti-vehicle defense or anti-fortification use.
6. **Stick grenades** stockpiled for when Americans close to hedgerow range.

### What This Means for the Player

**The kill chain:**
- MG-42 pins the player's platoon in the open
- Mortar fire begins dropping on the pinned troops
- Riflemen pick off anyone who tries to move
- If the player attempts to flank, the second MG catches the flanking element
- If the player closes to grenade range, stick grenades come over the hedgerow
- If the player brings vehicles, Panzerfausts wait at the gaps

**Breaking the chain:**
- Smoke to blind the MG-42
- Suppressive fire (BAR, M1919 if available) to keep the MG crew's heads down
- Flanking element moves laterally, not forward, to get outside the MG's beaten zone
- Grenades and Gammon bombs over the hedgerow once close enough
- Kill the mortar observer to stop indirect fire adjustment
- Speed and violence once committed — a slow assault bleeds out under mortar fire

This defensive system is why Normandy hedgerow fighting was the bloodiest close combat of the Western Front. Every field was a potential kill zone. Every hedgerow could hide a strongpoint. The player must learn to read terrain, identify threats by sound, and execute fire-and-movement under pressure — or lose men in every engagement.
